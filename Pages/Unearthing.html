A Traction: Unearthing Repair

The work reflects the studio as a performative space, where surfaces function as a language, absorbing energy and sound over time. Through digging, layering, and listening to the ambient sound of audiobooks, it embeds performance within the artistic process, reflecting on the solitude of labour and ongoing negotiation within the space.


Amna Ilyas

Amna Ilyas is a multidisciplinary artist and art educator. She holds an MA in Fine Art from the UCA (OCA), London. She has exhibited solo projects both at national and international venues, including Bayt Al Mamzar, Dubai (2023), Aicon Gallery in London (2011), and Seven Art (now known as Nature Morte) in New Delhi, India (2010). She has been awarded several fellowships for residencies and research programs globally, notable institutions such as the Vermont Studios Center in the USA, Edinburgh Sculpture Workshop in Scotland, Northlands Creative Glass Sculpture in the UK (2012), Kempinski Art Program in Slovakia, Austria, Berlin and The Hungarian Multicultural Center in Budapest (2019).

Her process-based practice explores the intersections of memory, materiality, and reconstruction. Informed by archaeological methods and conservation techniques, her approach is both gestural and investigative—where excavation and reconstruction extend a relationship between various surfaces and sound, discovering that they can not be separated.

Thinking of the studio as a performative space, she engages with the studio as both a subject and a site of process where she treats surfaces as a language that can be seductive, transparent, or opaque. These surfaces function as institutions, absorbing layers of energy, dialogue, and presence over time.

The processes of digging, layering, and listening shaped by the ambient sound of Audiobooks create an echoing dialogue to rephrase through gestures. This recursive approach reverberates Bruce Nauman’s assertion that “if I am an artist and I am in the studio, then whatever I am doing in the studio must be art.”

Through repetition, appropriation, sound, and the presence of her own body, she embeds performance into the artistic process, reflecting on the solitude of labour within a space—an ongoing negotiation she constructs for herself.
